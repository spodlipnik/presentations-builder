# Pacing Guide: Timing Templates for Presentations

## General Pacing Principles

Pacing is not about speaking speed — it is about the rhythm of information delivery. Uniform pacing is the most common mistake presenters make. Audiences read it as monotony, disengage, and stop processing.

**Attention drops after 10 minutes.** Neuroscientist John Medina (Brain Rules) documents that sustained attention begins to degrade around the 10-minute mark. Plan an explicit attention reset at every 10-minute interval: a story, a question, a prop, a modality shift, a striking image. Do not assume continued engagement — engineer it.

**Bookend heavy.** Research on primacy and recency effects consistently shows that audiences remember what came first and what came last far better than what came in the middle. Place your most important content in the first 20% and final 20% of the talk. The middle is for depth, not for debuts.

**Slow down for:**
- Key findings and conclusions (give the audience time to absorb)
- Emotionally resonant moments (rushing them neutralizes impact)
- Complex concepts or technical data (compression causes confusion)

**Speed up for:**
- Background information the audience likely already knows
- Obvious context and setup (do not make experts wait)
- High-energy narrative passages where momentum serves the story

**Pause deliberately:**
- After a key point — silence signals "this matters, hold it"
- Before transitions — a beat of stillness frames what is coming
- After rhetorical questions — give the audience time to internally respond

## 10-Minute Talk Template

**Goal:** Deliver one main finding with clarity and confidence. Nothing more.

```
Opening hook:           0:00-0:45    (1 slide)
Context / Problem:      0:45-2:30    (2-3 slides)
Key Method (brief):     2:30-4:00    (2 slides)
Core Results:           4:00-7:30    (3-4 slides)
Significance:           7:30-9:00    (1-2 slides)
Close:                  9:00-9:30    (1 slide)
Buffer:                 9:30-10:00
Total: 8-10 slides. ONE main finding only.
```

**Structural notes:**
- The hook must earn attention within 30 seconds — a question, a number, a contradiction
- Methods get a maximum of 90 seconds; the audience does not need to replicate your work
- Core results are your talk — do not rush them; spend 45-60 seconds per results slide
- Significance answers "so what?" — make the answer explicit, not implied
- The close restates your single finding and leaves one memorable phrase

**What to cut at this duration:**
- All literature review slides (weave references into spoken content only)
- Limitations slide (mention one verbally if essential; do not give it a slide)
- Any result not directly supporting the main finding
- Any methods detail the audience cannot act on

**Rehearsal target:** 9 minutes flat. The 30-second buffer is not negotiable dead air — it is your protection against a stumble, a question from the chair, or a slow transition.

## 15-Minute Talk Template

**Goal:** Deliver 2-3 key messages with one STAR moment (Something They'll Always Remember).

```
Hook + Context:         0:00-2:30    (2-3 slides)
Problem / Gap:          2:30-4:00    (1-2 slides)
Methods (selective):    4:00-6:30    (2-3 slides)
Results:                6:30-11:00   (4-5 slides)
Discussion:             11:00-13:30  (1-2 slides)
Close:                  13:30-14:30  (1 slide)
Buffer:                 14:30-15:00
Total: 12-15 slides. 2-3 key messages. One STAR moment.
```

**Structural notes:**
- Hook and context can be combined — open with a problem statement that doubles as context
- The gap slide is critical: audiences need to understand why this work needed doing
- Methods should be selective; include only what is necessary to trust the results
- Build the results section in order of narrative logic, not chronological order of discovery
- Place the STAR moment at the peak of the results section, around minutes 9-10
- Discussion should interpret, not repeat — avoid restating what the results slide already showed

**What to cut at this duration:**
- Sub-analyses that do not support any of the 3 key messages
- Acknowledgements slide (thank collaborators verbally or in a footer)
- Detailed statistical tables (use figures; tables belong in supplementary material)
- Any results slide without a spoken interpretation sentence

**Attention reset:** At the 10-minute mark, deploy the STAR moment — a striking visual, a surprising number, or a brief human story. This is not decoration; it is a structural intervention against attention decay.

## 20-Minute Talk Template

**Goal:** Full narrative arc with two results sections, an attention reset, and a clear STAR moment.

```
Opening story/hook:     0:00-2:30    (2 slides)
Context + Problem:      2:30-5:00    (2-3 slides)
Methods:                5:00-8:00    (3 slides)
Results Section 1:      8:00-11:00   (3-4 slides)
Attention Reset:        ~10:00       (built into results or as transition beat)
STAR Moment:            ~13:00       (1 slide)
Results Section 2:      11:00-15:00  (3-4 slides)
Implications:           15:00-17:30  (2 slides)
Close:                  17:30-19:00  (1 slide)
Buffer:                 19:00-20:00
Total: 15-20 slides. Attention reset at 10 min mark.
```

**Structural notes:**
- Open with a story, not a title slide read aloud — the audience already has the program
- Split results into two sections with a clear conceptual hinge between them (e.g., "what we found" vs. "what it means for X")
- The attention reset at minute 10 can be a brief anecdote, a provocative question, or a full-slide image — it does not need to be a separate slide if the STAR moment is placed there
- Implications should be concrete: who benefits, what changes, what the field should do next
- The close must contain a single, memorable sentence — the one thing you want in the audience's notes

**What to cut at this duration:**
- A third results section — save it for a journal paper
- Extended methodology justifications — one sentence of rationale per method choice is enough
- Hedging language on your implications slide — own the significance of your work

## 30-Minute Talk Template

**Goal:** Three-section structure, each with its own mini-arc. Full narrative development.

```
Opening story/hook:     0:00-3:00    (2 slides)
Roadmap:                3:00-4:00    (1 slide, "Three things today")
Section 1:              4:00-12:00   (5-6 slides, own mini-arc)
Transition:             12:00-12:30
Attention Reset:        ~10:00       (story, question, or modality change within Section 1)
Section 2:              12:30-20:00  (5-6 slides)
STAR Moment:            ~20:00       (1 slide)
Transition:             20:00-20:30
Section 3:              20:30-25:00  (4-5 slides)
Synthesis + Close:      25:00-28:00  (2 slides)
Buffer:                 28:00-30:00
Total: 20-25 slides. 3 sections, each with own narrative arc.
```

**Structural notes:**
- The roadmap slide is mandatory at this duration — tell the audience the three things before you tell them the three things; it creates cognitive scaffolding and reduces fatigue
- Each section must have: a setup (what we are doing here), a body (the content), and a landing (what we just learned)
- Attention resets are required at minute 10 and minute 20 — plan them explicitly, not reactively
- The STAR moment belongs at minute 20, not the end — it gives the audience energy for the final section
- Synthesis should connect across all three sections, not recap each one sequentially
- The close should feel like a landing, not a halt — it earns the applause

**What to cut at this duration:**
- A fourth section — restructure as a sub-section instead
- Any slide that serves as a placeholder ("Now let's look at...") without delivering content
- Overly granular timelines or process diagrams — use a single simplified version
- Defensive slides (alternate explanations, extensive limitations) — address in Q&A instead

## Slide Density Guidelines

These are rates per individual slide, not averages across the deck. Apply them slide-by-slide when building your timing plan.

| Slide Type              | Time Allocation  |
|-------------------------|------------------|
| Minimum (any slide)     | 45 seconds       |
| Average                 | 60–90 seconds    |
| Data-heavy slide        | 90–120 seconds   |
| Emotional / image slide | 30–45 seconds    |
| Title / transition      | 10–15 seconds    |

**Practical application:** Count your slides. Multiply by 75 seconds (the midpoint average). If the result exceeds your allotted time by more than 10%, cut slides before you cut content — a crowded deck at speed communicates nothing.

For data-heavy slides: budget an extra 30 seconds beyond the data itself for interpretation. Show the result; then say what it means. Audiences do not self-interpret graphs under time pressure.

For emotional and image slides: resist the urge to fill the silence with narration. Let the image work. Speak less, pause more. The slide earns its own time.
